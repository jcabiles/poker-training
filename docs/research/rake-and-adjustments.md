# Rake and How It Changes Correct Strategy

Scope: NLHE cash, ~100bb effective, LIVE $2/$3 focus (US cardrooms). Compiled for poker-math calibration. Every numeric claim is tagged [SOLVED/derivable], [SOURCED estimate], or [HEURISTIC/uncertain] and cited.

## 1. Typical live $2/$3 rake structure

- **Percentage rake with a cap is the dominant live model.** Typical live cardroom rake runs **5–10% of the pot**, capped at a maximum dollar amount regardless of pot size. [SOURCED estimate] — [SplitSuit, "Live Poker Cash Game Truths"](https://www.splitsuit.com/live-poker-cash-game-truths); [PokerCoaching, "How to Beat Rake in Poker"](https://pokercoaching.com/blog/rake-in-poker/)
- **Representative low-stakes cap: ~$4–$6, sometimes with a "promo drop" on top** (e.g., "$5+$1" = $5 normal rake cap + $1 promotion/bad-beat-jackpot drop). SplitSuit's worked example: a $34 pot under a "$5+$1" structure is raked $3 (10% up to the cap) + $1 promo = $4 total. [SOURCED estimate] — [SplitSuit](https://www.splitsuit.com/live-poker-cash-game-truths)
- **"No flop, no drop" is standard at most US rooms**: if the hand ends preflop (everyone folds to a raise, or a 3-bet takes it down), no rake is taken. Some rooms — SplitSuit specifically flags **some California cardrooms** — instead rake on any hand regardless of whether a flop is seen; these are considered worse games and best avoided. [SOURCED estimate] — [SplitSuit](https://www.splitsuit.com/live-poker-cash-game-truths); [Wikipedia, "Rake (poker)"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rake_(poker))
- **A distinct, common live variant: flat "collection" / "drop" fee instead of pot-percentage rake.** Many California cardrooms use a fixed per-hand collection fee (sometimes scaled by number of players) taken from the table before or after the first bet, rather than a percentage of the pot. Example from an actual regulatory filing (Poker Flats Casino drop schedule, filed with the CA Attorney General): $1–$2 stakes = $2 drop; $2–$4 = $3 drop; $3–$6 = $3 drop, drop taken before the start of the hand. [SOLVED/derivable — primary source, verbatim filed schedule] — [CA DOJ / Poker Flats Casino drop structure filing](https://oag.ca.gov/sites/all/files/agweb/pdfs/gambling/pokerflats.pdf) (PDF, CA Attorney General gambling filings)
  - A separately reported example: a flat $6 drop taken on every hand that sees a flop at San Jose-area $1/2 and $1/3 games. [SOURCED estimate, single-source] — cited within [WebSearch results on $2/$3 rake structures; original claim from player-reported forum discussion, not independently re-verified]
- **Higher-limit live games often shift to time-based rake** (a fee collected every ~30 minutes per player, or from the pot once above a threshold) instead of, or blended with, percentage rake — typically at $10/$20 and above, not at $2/$3. [SOURCED estimate] — [Wikipedia, "Rake (poker)"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rake_(poker))
- **Live rake is meaningfully higher than online rake as a fraction of the pot at comparable stakes.** Online cash rake is usually 3–5% capped; live is usually 5–10% capped, and live caps hit sooner relative to pot size at low stakes because dollar caps are flat while stacks are smaller. [SOURCED estimate] — [Wikipedia, "Rake (poker)"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rake_(poker)); [PrimeDope rake comparison](https://www.primedope.com/online-poker-rake-comparison-rake-calculator/)
- **Caps shrink as a fraction of the pot at higher stakes**, meaning the *effective* rake percentage (rake ÷ pot, in equilibrium-sized pots) is much steeper at $1/$2–$2/$3 than at $5/$10+. Illustrative example: a $100/$200 game's ~$25–$50 cap is about a quarter of one big blind, vs. roughly 2.5 big blinds of cap at the lowest live stakes. [SOURCED estimate] — [PokerCoaching, "How to Beat Rake in Poker"](https://pokercoaching.com/blog/rake-in-poker/)

**Practical takeaway for a $2/$3 calibration spec**: model live $2/$3 rake as roughly **~5–10% of the pot, capped around $4–$6** (some rooms $5+$1 promo), no-flop-no-drop at most rooms but not universally (flat per-hand "drop" games exist, especially in CA). Treat exact numbers as venue-specific and use this as a representative range, not a fixed constant. [HEURISTIC/uncertain — ranges synthesized from multiple secondary sources plus one primary filing for a single CA room; no single source gives a canonical "$2/$3 average"]

## 2. How rake mathematically tightens ranges

### 2.1 Mechanism (uncontroversial, solved math)
Rake reduces the effective size of every pot a player wins, which (a) reduces the pot odds offered on a call, i.e., raises the raw equity needed to profitably continue, and (b) reduces the EV of every marginal continuation (open, call, or 3-bet call) that relies on realizing equity in a multi-way or postflop pot. Because rake is *not* charged on pots won preflop uncontested (no-flop-no-drop), rake asymmetrically penalizes speculative, equity-realization-dependent lines relative to hands that can win the pot outright preflop. [SOLVED/derivable] — [Upswing Poker, "What is Rake in Poker & How Does It Impact Strategy?"](https://upswingpoker.com/rake-poker-strategy-adjustments/)

### 2.2 Quantified equity-needed shift (worked examples)
Upswing's worked example at $1/2 with a $5-capped rake:
- **Big blind defense**, no rake: **~30% raw equity needed** to profitably continue vs. a raise; with the $5 cap rake applied to that pot size: **~35% raw equity needed** — roughly a **5 percentage-point tightening**. [SOURCED estimate] — [Upswing Poker, rake strategy adjustments](https://upswingpoker.com/rake-poker-strategy-adjustments/)
- A second cited example in the same family of analysis (calling a 3-bet, CO vs. BU) showed a swing from **~30% to ~35%** raw equity needed once rake is applied — consistent order of magnitude (~5 points) for a single $5 rake charge on a modestly sized pot. [SOURCED estimate] — [Upswing Poker](https://upswingpoker.com/rake-poker-strategy-adjustments/)
- Note: a separate secondary source (GTO Wizard–adjacent commentary encountered during search, not independently re-verified against the original Upswing figures) cited a larger 30%→50% swing for BB defense under a $5 cap; the two figures for "BB defense equity needed" are not fully consistent across summarized sources. **Treat the exact percentage-point magnitude as approximate — order of magnitude is "several points of raw equity," not a fixed number.** [HEURISTIC/uncertain — conflicting secondary summaries of the same source]
- The rake's *bite* shrinks as pots get larger relative to the cap: in Upswing's example, $5 rake on a $37 pot is **13.5% of the pot**, versus a much higher percentage bite on a smaller preflop-sized pot — meaning **rake distorts small/marginal pots far more than big ones**, which is exactly why marginal (not premium) hands are what get cut. [SOLVED/derivable] — [Upswing Poker](https://upswingpoker.com/rake-poker-strategy-adjustments/)

### 2.3 Effect on RFI, calling, and blind-defense ranges specifically
- **RFI**: solvers cut the bottom of the opening range because those hands' thin postflop equity-realization edge no longer clears the rake tax; simultaneously, rake creates a small incentive to open wider with hands that can *win preflop outright* (blockers, hands that fold out easily) since preflop-won pots are rake-free. GTO Wizard specifically notes rake-adjusted solvers can prefer a hand like A5o (raises fold equity / blocks 3-bets) over 65s (better realized postflop equity) at the margin — a *shape* change, not just a tightening. [SOURCED estimate] — [GTO Wizard, "How To Respond to Large Preflop Raises in Poker"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/how-to-respond-to-large-preflop-raises-in-poker/); search-summarized GTO Wizard preflop-morphology content
- **Calling ranges collapse much more than opening/3-betting ranges.** Under a steep example rake (10% capped at 2bb, i.e., unusually harsh — steeper than typical live $2/3), GTO Wizard's cited solver output has cold-calling become "nearly non-existent except from the big blind," with the solver folding hands as strong as **88 on the button to a 2x open from CO**. This is a stress-test example (harsh rake), not representative of typical live $2/3, but it demonstrates the *direction and magnitude* of the effect: rake pushes the solver toward a **raise-or-fold** structure and away from flatting, because "any hand strong enough to overcome the edge lost to rake is strong enough to raise." [SOURCED estimate, extreme-case example] — [GTO Wizard, "How To Respond to Large Preflop Raises"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/how-to-respond-to-large-preflop-raises-in-poker/)
- **Blind defense (especially BB) tightens more than opening ranges do.** Multiple sources agree the tightening is "even more profound" for blind defense than for RFI, because the blinds are the players most exposed to multiway, rake-taxed, equity-realization-dependent pots. [SOURCED estimate] — [GTO Wizard blog, search-summarized preflop-rake content]; [Upswing Poker, "2 Winning Ways to Adjust Your Preflop Strategy in Low Stakes Live Poker"](https://upswingpoker.com/live-preflop-rake-adjustments/)
- **Stake-dependent magnitude**: GTO Wizard compared NL50 (larger rake cap relative to stack, e.g. ~4bb cap cited) vs. NL500 (much smaller relative cap, ~0.6bb cited): BB folded **62%** vs. a late-position open at NL50, vs. **59%** at NL500 — a modest but real (~3 percentage point) *widening* of BB defense as the rake cap becomes negligible relative to stack size. This directly supports scaling the calibration spec's rake penalty by (cap ÷ effective stack) rather than treating rake as a flat percentage independent of stakes. [SOURCED estimate] — [GTO Wizard, "Rake & Rakeback Explained"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/rake-rakeback-explained-optimize-your-poker-earnings/)
- **No-flop-no-drop vs. rake-preflop-too, empirical comparison**: at matched NL50 stakes, a traditional no-flop-no-drop site showed CO raising 8% / calling 1.4%, vs. a site that rakes preflop pots too (GGPoker, per this source) showing CO raising 9% / calling 3.2% — i.e., **removing the preflop rake exemption reduced raising and roughly halved-to-a-third'd relative preflop calling frequency** in the exempt condition. This is the cleanest available quantified evidence that the *no-flop-no-drop rule itself*, not just rake's magnitude, materially changes correct preflop frequencies. [SOURCED estimate] — [GTO Wizard, "Rake & Rakeback Explained"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/rake-rakeback-explained-optimize-your-poker-earnings/)

## 3. "Rake-adjusted" solver solutions vs. rake-free GTO — what moves most

- **GTO Wizard offers purpose-built rake-adjusted solves**: preflop-only solutions across a wide range of rake structures from NL25 to NL10k, and custom-raked postflop solves (currently limited to heads-up postflop spots) where the user can input their own venue's rake % and cap. This is presented as the standard tool for seeing exactly how a given room's rake reshapes optimal frequencies. [SOLVED/derivable — product documentation] — [GTO Wizard, "Customizable Raked Solutions With GTO Wizard AI"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/customizable-raked-solutions-with-gto-wizard-ai/); [GTO Wizard Help, "How To Build Custom Solutions"](https://help.gtowizard.com/how-to-build-custom-solutions/)
- **What moves most, in rank order per the sources reviewed**:
  1. **Small blind limping / completing** — described as "the most condoned leak in poker," and specifically penalized by rake: completing the SB doesn't close the action, invites a multiway rake-taxed pot, and leaves the SB with the worst position postflop. Solvers instead prefer SB to fold or attack with a raise/3-bet (which has a chance to win the pot preflop rake-free). [SOURCED estimate] — [CardPlayer, "More Rake Is Not Better For A Wider Range"](https://www.cardplayer.com/poker-news/1641215-poker-strategy-more-rake-is-not-better-for-a-wider-range) (content summarized via search; direct fetch was blocked, see note below); consistent with [Upswing Poker, live preflop rake adjustments](https://upswingpoker.com/live-preflop-rake-adjustments/)
  2. **Marginal cold-calls, especially multiway/limped pots** — as in §2.3, calling ranges compress toward "raise or fold," and limped multiway pots specifically are flagged as unattractive under rake because players "pay fees more often while realizing equity less cleanly" in exactly the low-SPR, multiway, equity-uncertain spots rake taxes hardest. [SOURCED estimate] — [CardPlayer](https://www.cardplayer.com/poker-news/1641215-poker-strategy-more-rake-is-not-better-for-a-wider-range) (search-summarized); [PokerCoaching, "How to Beat Rake"](https://pokercoaching.com/blog/rake-in-poker/)
  3. **Big blind defense** — tightens more than RFI per §2.3, but stays wide relative to SB because BB is closing the action and getting the best price. [SOURCED estimate] — as cited above
  4. **RFI ranges** — tighten the least of the three, and change *shape* (favoring blockers/fold-equity hands over pure postflop-playability hands) as much as they shrink in size. [SOURCED estimate] — [GTO Wizard, "How To Respond to Large Preflop Raises"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/how-to-respond-to-large-preflop-raises-in-poker/)
- **3-betting is relatively rake-favored**: because a successful preflop 3-bet (either taking the pot down uncontested or getting to a heads-up, larger pot where the rake cap is a smaller fraction of the pot) avoids or dilutes the rake tax, rake mildly *increases* the incentive to 3-bet relative to flatting, reinforcing the raise-or-fold shift described above. [SOURCED estimate] — [Upswing Poker, rake strategy adjustments](https://upswingpoker.com/rake-poker-strategy-adjustments/); [CardPlayer](https://www.cardplayer.com/poker-news/1641215-poker-strategy-more-rake-is-not-better-for-a-wider-range)
- **Caveat on the CardPlayer source**: direct WebFetch of cardplayer.com returned HTTP 403 (blocked); the claims attributed to it above come from Claude's web-search result summarization, not a direct fetch of the article text. Treat this specific source's claims as slightly lower-confidence than the directly-fetched sources. [HEURISTIC/uncertain — sourcing caveat]

## 4. Rules of thumb: bb/100 cost of rake, break-even adjustments

- **General framing**: "the higher the rake, the higher your win rate has to be to overcome it." A concrete illustrative example given: a player beating the game **7bb/100** before rake, paying **5bb/100** in rake, nets only **2bb/100** — i.e., rake consumed roughly **71% of gross winrate** in that example. [SOURCED estimate, illustrative example not a universal constant] — [GTO Wizard, "Rake & Rakeback Explained"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/rake-rakeback-explained-optimize-your-poker-earnings/)
- **Online rake-cost benchmarks by stake** (useful as an anchor even though this spec is live-focused, since it's the best-sourced quantified table found):
  - 2NL full ring: **~8 bb/100**; 2NL 6-max: **~10.4 bb/100**
  - 100NL full ring: **~5.15 bb/100** (rake reported as consuming **63.2%** of that player's gross profit in the cited example)
  - 200NL full ring: **~3.77 bb/100**; 200NL 6-max: **~4.57 bb/100**
  - [SOURCED estimate] — [Rakeback.com, "Comparing Rake at Different Stakes"](https://www.rakeback.com/poker-rake/game-stakes/)
  - Key structural point from the same source: because most sites cap rake at a **flat dollar amount**, the *bb-denominated* cost of rake falls as stakes rise — i.e., rake is proportionally much more punishing at micro/small stakes than mid/high stakes. This is the online analogue of the live-cap-shrinks-as-%-of-pot point in §1. [SOLVED/derivable from cap structure]
- **No live-specific $2/$3 bb/100 rake-cost figure was found in the sources reviewed.** Live rake-cost-in-bb/100 numbers are much less publicly documented than online (no live site publishes hand-history-derived rake reports the way online HUD/tracking tools do for online rake). **This is a gap** — the calibration spec should either (a) derive an estimated live bb/100 rake cost analytically from the $2/$3 structure in §1 (pot size × rake % × flop-seen frequency, capped), or (b) state the live number as an assumption/estimate rather than a sourced fact. [HEURISTIC/uncertain — no direct source found]
- **Break-even / ROI framing (tournament-derived, generalizable in spirit only)**: Break-even ROI = rake ÷ prize-pool contribution; example given, an 8% rake fee roughly halves an otherwise-15%-ROI edge. This is a tournament-buy-in framing, not directly transferable to cash bb/100, but illustrates the general point that rake consumes a large, non-trivial fraction of a small-to-moderate skill edge. [SOURCED estimate, tournament context — not directly applicable to cash bb/100 math] — [LegalUSPokerSites, "Poker Tournament Rake: How Fees Impact Your Profitability"](https://www.legaluspokersites.com/blogs/tournament-rake-structures/)
- **"1 bb/100 of rake difference compounds into thousands of bb over a year" / rake as the single biggest game-selection factor for small-stakes grinders** — directionally correct and widely repeated, but stated as a general principle rather than tied to a specific derivation in the sources found. [HEURISTIC/uncertain] — synthesized from [Beasts of Poker, "What is bb/100 in Poker?"](https://beastsofpoker.com/what-is-bb-100/) and [GTO Wizard, "Rake & Rakeback Explained"](https://blog.gtowizard.com/rake-rakeback-explained-optimize-your-poker-earnings/)
